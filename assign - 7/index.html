Albert Einstein (14 March 1879 – 18 April 1955) was a German-born theoretical physicist who is widely held to be one of the greatest and most influential scientists of all time. Best known for developing the theory of relativity, Einstein also made important contributions to quantum mechanics, and was thus a central figure in the revolutionary reshaping of the scientific understanding of nature that modern physics accomplished in the first decades of the twentieth century.

His mass–energy equivalence formula E = mc2, which arises from relativity theory, has been dubbed "the world's most famous equation". He received the 1921 Nobel Prize in Physics "for his services to theoretical physics, and especially for his discovery of the law of the photoelectric effect", a pivotal step in the development of quantum theory. His work is also known for its influence on the philosophy of science.

Biography

Early life and education

Albert Einstein was born in Ulm, in the Kingdom of Württemberg, German Empire, on 14 March 1879 into a family of secular Ashkenazi Jews. His parents were Hermann Einstein, a salesman and engineer, and Pauline Koch. In 1880, the family moved to Munich, where Einstein's father and his uncle Jakob founded Elektrotechnische Fabrik J. Einstein & Cie, a company that manufactured electrical equipment based on direct current.

Marriages and children

The correspondence between Einstein and Mileva Marić, a fellow student at ETH, was discovered and published in 1987. It revealed that the couple had a daughter, "Lieserl", born in early 1902 in Novi Sad where Marić was staying with her parents. Marić had returned to Switzerland without the child, whose real name and fate are unknown. Einstein and Marić married in January 1903. In May 1904, their son Hans Albert Einstein was born in Bern, Switzerland. Their son Eduard was born in Zürich in July 1910.

Scientific career

Throughout his life, Einstein published hundreds of scientific papers and over 150 non-scientific works. On 30 June 1905, Einstein completed his paper "On the Electrodynamics of Moving Bodies", which introduced the theory of special relativity. In the same year, he submitted his thesis "A New Determination of Molecular Dimensions", which earned him a PhD. This year is often referred to as his "annus mirabilis" (miracle year) because he published four groundbreaking papers.

Annus mirabilis papers

In 1905, often called his "miracle year", Einstein published four groundbreaking papers. These outlined the theory of the photoelectric effect, explained Brownian motion, introduced special relativity, and demonstrated mass-energy equivalence. These papers were foundational to modern physics and transformed our understanding of the universe.

Political and humanitarian work

Einstein was a passionate pacifist and a supporter of civil rights. After moving to the United States to flee the Nazi regime, he became a vocal critic of racism and segregation. He famously called racism "America's worst disease." In 1939, alarmed by the rise of Nazi Germany, he and fellow physicist Leó Szilárd signed a letter to President Franklin D. Roosevelt, warning of the potential development of "extremely powerful bombs of a new type" and recommending that the U.S. begin similar research. This letter was a key factor in the establishment of the Manhattan Project.

"Imagination is more important than knowledge. For knowledge is limited, whereas imagination embraces the entire world, stimulating progress, giving birth to evolution."

Later in life, Einstein deeply regretted his letter and became a strong advocate for nuclear disarmament and world government. He collaborated with Bertrand Russell on the Russell–Einstein Manifesto, which highlighted the dangers of nuclear weapons and called on world leaders to seek peaceful resolutions to international conflict.

Legacy

Einstein's legacy in physics is immense. His theories of relativity fundamentally changed our understanding of space, time, gravity, and the universe. His contributions to quantum theory laid the groundwork for numerous technological advancements, including lasers and semiconductors. Beyond science, Einstein became a global icon and a symbol of genius and intellectual curiosity. The element einsteinium was named in his honor, and his name is synonymous with genius.

Awards and honors

Einstein received numerous awards and honors, including the 1921 Nobel Prize in Physics. He was also awarded the Copley Medal (1925) from the Royal Society and the Max Planck Medal (1929) from the German Physical Society. He was a member of many of the world's leading scientific academies, reflecting the profound impact of his work on the global scientific community.
